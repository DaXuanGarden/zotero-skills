## 1. Bottom Line

The evidence base supports a **cautiously worded multisystem interpretation** of the manuscript paragraph. The strongest external support is for: 

- **Genetic epidemiology of LST/sedentary behavior:** large GWAS evidence identifies leisure screen time (LST)-associated loci and skeletal-muscle-relevant enrichment.
- **LST–PCOS association:** recent Mendelian randomization (MR) studies report that genetically proxied leisure screen time is associated with increased PCOS risk.
- **PCOS as a complex multisystem disorder:** high-quality reviews support PCOS as a polygenic, heterogeneous syndrome involving reproductive, neuroendocrine and metabolic mechanisms.
- **Neuroendocrine and reproductive axes:** strong review evidence supports hypothalamic-pituitary/GnRH-LH dysregulation, ovarian androgen excess, adrenal contribution and impaired steroid feedback.
- **Metabolic pathways:** strong evidence supports insulin resistance, hyperinsulinaemia and metabolic dysfunction in PCOS; this supports interpretation of insulin/metabolic pathway enrichment.

However, some specific claims require caution:

- **Cerebellar enrichment** appears to come from the present study’s enrichment results rather than from directly matching external literature.
- **Exact PI3K-Akt and AMPK pathway enrichment** in shared LST–PCOS architecture is not directly established by the retrieved papers; external evidence supports broader insulin/metabolic dysregulation.
- **Immune dysregulation** is only weakly/indirectly supported by the retrieved evidence and should not be foregrounded unless additional citations are added.

**Recommended stance:** cite the present analyses as the source of pathway/tissue findings, and use external studies to support biological plausibility and interpretation rather than to imply independent replication of every enrichment signal.

## 2. Recommended Manuscript Text

> Functional enrichment analyses in the present study indicated that the shared genetic architecture between LST and PCOS extends across several interconnected biological systems rather than being confined to the ovary. This interpretation is consistent with GWAS evidence that LST-related loci show tissue-relevant enrichment in skeletal muscle and with MR evidence linking genetically proxied leisure screen time to increased PCOS risk [1–3]. In PCOS, prior genomic and mechanistic reviews support a complex, polygenic disorder involving reproductive, neuroendocrine and metabolic abnormalities, including hypothalamic-pituitary/GnRH-LH dysfunction, hyperandrogenism, insulin resistance and ovarian-adrenal contributions [4–8]. Accordingly, our pathway and tissue-specific findings support a multisystem framework in which sedentary behavior and PCOS may converge through coordinated neuroendocrine, metabolic and reproductive dysregulation, while pathway-specific signals such as cerebellar localization, PI3K-Akt/AMPK enrichment and immune involvement require cautious interpretation and further validation.

## 8. Integrated Writing Advice

1. **Separate study findings from external evidence.**  
   The enrichment/tissue results should be attributed to “the present study.” External literature should support interpretation, not be presented as if it independently found the same LST–PCOS shared enrichment.

2. **Use “consistent with” rather than “demonstrates.”**  
   Suggested verbs: “consistent with,” “supports the interpretation,” “biologically plausible,” “aligns with.” Avoid “proves,” “confirms,” or “establishes” for pathway-specific findings.

3. **Tone down exact pathway claims.**  
   PI3K-Akt and AMPK can remain as enrichment results, but the citation support should be described as broader insulin/metabolic pathway evidence unless additional pathway-specific references are added.

4. **Do not overstate cerebellar support.**  
   Keep cerebellar localization as a study-derived tissue-specific signal and identify it as requiring further validation.

5. **Reconsider immune dysregulation wording.**  
   If no further immune-specific sources are added, revise “neuroendocrine, metabolic, immune and reproductive dysregulation” to “neuroendocrine, metabolic and reproductive dysregulation,” or write “with possible immune involvement requiring further validation.”

6. **Mention MR limitations where appropriate.**  
   MR studies support genetic liability/proxy associations, but estimates can vary by instrument selection, population, pleiotropy control and phenotype definitions.
