# Evidence Review: Sedentary Behavior, LST GWAS, and PCOS Genetic Architecture

## Bottom Line

The draft's central idea is plausible but should be framed cautiously. Current evidence supports: (1) sedentary-behavior traits such as leisure screen time (LST) are heritable and have genome-wide significant loci; (2) PCOS has a substantial and expanding GWAS locus map; and (3) PCOS genetic liability is linked to adiposity, obesity/insulin resistance, hormonal, inflammatory, and metabolic pathways. However, the inspected evidence does **not** directly demonstrate that sedentary behavior and PCOS share causal variants through horizontal pleiotropy. The manuscript should therefore present direct sedentary behavior–PCOS pleiotropy as an unresolved knowledge gap rather than an established fact.

Two draft claims require correction or hedging:

- Replace the unsupported “88 genome-wide significant loci” statement with the evidence-supported formulation that Wang et al. identified **99 loci associated with MVPA, LST, and/or sedentary behavior at work**.
- Remove or hedge “brain enrichment”; inspected evidence supports skeletal-muscle enrichment for LST-associated loci, but brain enrichment was not confirmed from the inspected metadata/abstract evidence.

## Recommended Manuscript Text

Genome-wide studies suggest that sedentary behavior and PCOS may have partly convergent, but not yet formally demonstrated shared, genetic and biological architectures. A large multi-ancestry GWAS meta-analysis of physical-activity and sedentary-behavior traits identified 99 loci associated with moderate-to-vigorous physical activity, leisure screen time (LST), and/or sedentary behavior at work, with LST-associated loci enriched for genes whose skeletal-muscle expression changes after resistance training (Wang et al., 2022). Independently, PCOS GWAS and genomic reviews have identified multiple susceptibility loci implicating gonadotropin/neuroendocrine regulation, while recent multi-ancestry GWAS expanded the PCOS locus map to 94 independent loci (Hayes et al., 2015; Dapas and Dunaif, 2022; Zhao et al., 2025). Cross-trait and clustering analyses further link PCOS liability to adiposity, obesity/insulin-resistance, and metabolic pathways (Liu et al., 2022; Stamou et al., 2024). These findings suggest convergence on metabolic and neuroendocrine biology, but whether sedentary behavior and PCOS share causal variants through horizontal pleiotropy remains unresolved. Testing this distinction could clarify whether sedentary behavior is genetically coupled to PCOS pathophysiology and prioritize pleiotropic loci and effector genes for mechanistic follow-up.

## Integrated Writing Advice

Use cautious, evidence-aligned language. The most reviewer-safe framing is:

- “partly convergent genetic and biological architectures” rather than “shared/common genetic architecture” as a settled fact;
- “sedentary-behavior and PCOS genetic findings converge on metabolic and neuroendocrine biology” rather than “sedentary behavior causes PCOS through shared loci”;
- “whether shared causal variants or horizontal pleiotropy exist remains unresolved” rather than “these traits are pleiotropically linked.”

Avoid overclaiming the sedentary-behavior side of the evidence. Wang et al. supports LST/sedentary-behavior GWAS and skeletal-muscle enrichment, but the inspected evidence does not establish direct PCOS overlap or brain enrichment. The PCOS side of the argument is stronger: modern PCOS GWAS and cross-trait studies robustly support genetic architecture involving gonadotropin/neuroendocrine regulation, adiposity, obesity/insulin resistance, and metabolic pathways.
